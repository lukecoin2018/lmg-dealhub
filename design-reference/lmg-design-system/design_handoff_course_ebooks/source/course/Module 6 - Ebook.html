The reframeChapter 6.0

Everything is negotiable.

In Module 4 you figured out what you're worth. This is where that number stops being a private calculation and becomes money in your account. There's a number on the table — and what you do next separates confident creators from the ones who accept whatever they're offered.

Here's the reframe: every part of a partnership is negotiable. Not just the fee — the deliverables, timeline, usage rights, exclusivity, payment terms, all of it. A first offer isn't a fixed reality. It's an opening position.

And the brands that become your best long-term partners actually respect creators who negotiate professionally — it signals you treat this as a business. This module gives you three things: how to walk in prepared, the tactics that move a deal, and the boundaries that tell you when to hold firm or walk.

You also have a tool — the Negotiation Assistant — that drafts professional replies for any stage. But we teach the logic first: on a live call there's no time to open an app. Confidence comes from preparation and a known walk-away number — not bravado.

Won before it beginsChapter 6.1

Know your worth before you say a word.

The single biggest predictor of how a negotiation goes is how prepared you walked in. Three things to lock down first — and the third is the one that gives you real power.

1
Knowledge is leverage

Market context

Know roughly what creators with your stats, niche, and engagement charge. Creator communities and rate-sharing groups are gold — the more you know what's normal, the harder you are to lowball.

2
Bring receipts

Performance proof

Your leverage made concrete. "My last three sponsored posts averaged 9% engagement and drove ~500 clicks" is a far stronger position than hoping they see your potential.

3
From Module 4 · your power

Your floor

Your absolute walk-away minimum. Knowing it cold is what gives you the nerve to politely walk — and the willingness to walk is your greatest source of power. Desperation weakens you; a known number strengthens you, and brands can feel the difference.

→ Prepared, not hopeful. Lock these three down and you negotiate every deal from strength.

What actually moves a dealChapter 6.2

The tactics that move a deal.

1+15–20% above target

Anchor high

Open above what you want — standard practice, not greed. It gives you room to concede and still land on your real number.

2The most important one

Trade variables

"Over budget"? Don't drop your rate — change what they get. Fewer deliverables, shorter usage, no exclusivity. Price is the last lever.

3Grow the deal

Bundle to add value

"One post $2,000; three over two months $5,400." Better per-post rate, sustained visibility — you held positioning and grew the total.

4Sweeten, don't shrink

Add value

An extra story, a longer window, BTS footage, a testimonial. Costs you little, feels substantial — keeps your core rate intact.

5 Use silence. State your rate, give one brief reason it's fair, then stop talking. Most creators panic in the pause and discount themselves. Don't. Let them respond — they'll often accept, or counter close to your target.
The rule you never break

Get everything in writing before any work begins — deliverables, payment amount & schedule, timeline, usage, exclusivity, revisions. A verbal "yes" is worth nothing if it's disputed. No contract from them? Send a summary email and ask them to confirm. This is your bridge into Module 7.

The same lines, over and overChapter 6.3

The seven objections you'll hear.

The pattern across all seven: don't drop your price reflexively, redirect to the variables you can trade, and frame your response as a professional standard — not a defensive reaction.

That's above our budget.
Don't drop price — trade. "Let's look at what we can adjust: deliverables, usage window, or exclusivity."
Other creators charge way less.
Don't take the bait. Educate: "Engagement & fit vary widely. My rate reflects 6.8% engagement and 82% your demographic."
We can only do $X maximum.
At/above floor? Accept, but ask for something back. Below floor? Trade scope. Won't work? Walk politely.
Can you be flexible on price?
A trap. Reframe: "I'm flexible on scope, timing, and usage. What matters most so we find a structure that works?"
We're offering great exposure.
A red flag. Polite + firm: "Exposure isn't a currency I can pay rent with. Professional brands compensate fairly."
Standard rate — take it or leave it.
Pressure, rarely true. Call it politely: "If there's no room on price, can we look at scope to find a fit?"
"I'll get back to you" … silence.
The stalled deal. Wait 3–5 days, follow up warmly, one final nudge a week later, then stop. Revisit in a few months.

Tactics flex · these don'tChapter 6.4

Non-negotiables & red flags.

Before any negotiation, know your hard lines — and frame them as professional policies, not personal refusals. "I maintain a policy of not granting unlimited usage rights without compensation that reflects that value" lands very differently than "no, I won't do that."

1Your floor

Minimum payment

The number below which the deal doesn't make business sense.

2Ties to Module 3

Creative control

You won't be fully scripted or made to claim things you can't stand behind.

3Without serious extra pay

Usage rights

No unlimited or perpetual use handed over for free.

4Protect your pipeline

Exclusivity

Nothing so long or broad it blocks too many other opportunities.

⚑ Red flags: won't discuss payment upfront · unlimited-everything for minimal pay · "exposure" instead of money · refuses to put anything in writing. These aren't bumps to push through — calmly decline and move on. More in Module 10.
