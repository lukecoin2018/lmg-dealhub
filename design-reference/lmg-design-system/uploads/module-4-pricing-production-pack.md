## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,200 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 4)

#### Why most creators undercharge

Pricing is the part of this business creators get most wrong, and almost always in the same direction: too low. There are three reasons, and naming them is the first step to fixing your rates.

First, creators anchor to a single number they heard somewhere — usually "a hundred dollars per ten thousand followers" — and treat it as a ceiling instead of a floor. It's a starting point, not the answer. Second, they price on follower count alone and ignore the thing that actually makes them valuable: engagement. And third — this is the big one — they give away usage rights, whitelisting, and exclusivity for free, because nobody told them those things have a price. They quote a number for "a post," and the brand walks away with the right to run that content as paid ads for a year, at no extra cost. That's not a small mistake. It's often the difference between a deal that's worth it and one that isn't.

This module fixes all three. You're going to understand exactly what sets your rate, learn to charge for everything that has value, and walk away able to defend your number when a brand pushes back. And you have a serious advantage: lmg.media's Rate Calculator does the math for you across four steps. But here's why we still teach the logic — because in a negotiation, a tool gives you a number, and understanding gives you the argument. We're making you fluent, not dependent. Let's go step by step, the same way the calculator does.

#### Step 1 — Your baseline: followers, niche, and engagement

Your baseline starts with reach, but it's shaped by two things that matter just as much.

The mental model is the familiar one: roughly a hundred dollars per ten thousand followers for a single post. So a creator with 50,000 followers starts around five hundred dollars. Treat that as a sanity-check floor, not your final number — because two factors move it significantly.

The first is your niche. Not all audiences are worth the same to brands. Finance, technology, and B2B niches command higher rates because the products are high-value and the audiences have real purchasing power. Lifestyle and fashion are more competitive — more creators chasing the same deals — which can press rates down. But specialization beats breadth everywhere: a focused "sustainable home" creator can out-earn a general lifestyle creator of the same size, because the audience is targeted and the expertise is clear. The calculator asks your niche on Step 1 for exactly this reason.

The second — and your biggest lever — is engagement. A creator whose audience actually interacts and buys is worth far more than a same-sized creator whose audience scrolls past. Use these benchmarks: 2–3% is average, 3–5% is good, and above 5% is excellent. If your engagement sits above average, you should price above the baseline, full stop. This is the number you calculated back in Module 1 — and this is where it finally sets your price. Some creators with smaller but highly engaged audiences charge more than larger, flatter accounts, and brands increasingly respect that.

#### Step 2 — Pricing your deliverables: platform and content type

Once you have a baseline, the next question is *what* you're actually making — because not all content costs you the same to produce or delivers the same value. The calculator's Step 2 has you build a package by adding deliverables, each with a platform and a content type, and there's real logic underneath those choices.

Platform matters. Instagram and TikTok often use similar baseline math, but YouTube commands a premium because the production time is higher and the content keeps working for months or years after posting — a single video can drive conversions long after a Reel has scrolled out of feeds.

Content type matters just as much. A static feed post is your baseline. Video — Reels and TikToks — generally runs one-and-a-half to two times a static post, because of the reach potential and the editing skill involved. Stories are cheaper, typically fifty to seventy percent of a feed post, and a set of three to five frames usually counts as one unit. Carousels and longer-form video sit accordingly. The calculator lets you mix all of these freely in one package, which mirrors how real deals work — a Reel plus a few Stories, say, rather than one thing in isolation.

When a brand wants several deliverables, you bundle them into a package. A modest package discount — ten to twenty percent — makes the deal attractive without gutting your rate. But be clear about exactly what's included, and don't discount so hard that you've undervalued the whole thing just to win it. A package should reward volume, not punish you for it.

#### Step 3 — Usage rights and terms: where the money hides

This is the step the calculator literally labels "where most creators undervalue themselves" — and it's right. Everything here is value you can charge for, and most creators don't.

Start with how the brand will use the content. **Organic only** — posted on your account, maybe reshared to theirs — is your baseline. **Paid advertising** — where they put media spend behind your content — is worth substantially more, because creator content in paid ads routinely outperforms traditional brand ads. If they're running your content as ads, that's a meaningful uplift, not a freebie.

Then there's **duration** — how long they can use it. The calculator offers 30 days, 90 days, 1 year, and perpetual. The longer the window, the more you charge, because you're handing over more value. And **perpetual is a red flag** — it means they own the use of your content forever; either price it like you're selling an asset (a large multiple of your rate) or decline.

**Whitelisting, or Spark Ads,** is its own line — and a big one. This is when the brand runs ads *from your account*, using your handle and your credibility so the ad looks like it came from you. That's your brand equity doing the work, and it should cost extra. The calculator adds 30% for it — treat that as your *floor*. When the brand plans significant ad spend, negotiate up toward one-and-a-half to two times your base rate, because that's the real market value of running ads from your handle. The +30% is the starting point; the ceiling is much higher.

**Exclusivity** is the last big lever. If a brand asks you not to work with their competitors, you're giving up income, and that should be paid for. Price it by length, and always pin down exactly what counts as a "competitor" so you don't accidentally lock yourself out of a whole category. Long stretches of exclusivity move you out of one-off pricing entirely and into ambassador territory — which is Module 9.

#### Beyond the calculator: rush, concepting, and scope

A couple of real value-adds sit outside the calculator's inputs, and you apply them manually. **Rush work** — a turnaround faster than your normal timeline — justifies a premium of twenty-five to fifty percent, because it disrupts your calendar and reshuffles your other commitments. **Concepting and strategy** — when a brand wants you to develop the creative idea or campaign direction, not just execute — is separate, billable work; some creators charge a flat concepting fee starting around five hundred dollars before any content is made.

And watch your **scope**, which is what the calculator's revision-round question is really about. Two rounds is standard, and three is your ceiling — beyond that you're producing multiple concepts for the price of one. The calculator flags four or more rounds as a warning sign; treat heavy revision demands as either a boundary to set or extra work to bill.

#### From a number to a defensible range

The calculator gives you a number. Your job is to turn it into a *range* you present with confidence. Rather than quoting one figure, give a range — for example, "a single Reel typically runs fifteen hundred to two thousand, depending on usage rights and timeline." The bottom of your range is your minimum for a straightforward project; the top accounts for rush turnaround, extensive usage, or complex deliverables. A range anchors the brand at your real value, gives you room to flex without seeming arbitrary, and signals that you understand different projects warrant different prices.

Know your floor before any conversation — the minimum below which a deal doesn't make sense for your business. And as a setup for negotiation: you'll open slightly above your target so you have room to move. We'll do that properly in Module 6. The calculator's Step 4 also nudges the deal-health basics — clear payment terms, fifty percent upfront, and never, ever accepting "exposure" as payment — which we'll put to work when we negotiate and when we spot red flags in Module 10.

#### Where this is going

You now know what sets your rate, how to charge for the things you used to give away, and how to present a number you can defend. That confidence changes everything about the next step — because in Module 5 we go pitch, and a creator who knows their worth pitches completely differently than one who's hoping. Run a real deal through the calculator, build your range, and bring it with you. You're about to put it in front of brands.

### Video 4.5 — From a Number to a Defensible Range
**Type:** Core · **Target:** 6–7 min

The calculator gives you a number. Now I'm going to show you how to turn that into something you present with total confidence — a range. Because how you *present* your price matters almost as much as the price itself.

[On screen: "Quote a range, not a number"]

Here's the shift. Instead of quoting one figure and hoping it's right, you quote a range. Something like: "A single Reel typically runs fifteen hundred to two thousand, depending on usage rights and timeline." That one sentence does three powerful things. Underneath the range is one number you keep to yourself: your floor.
