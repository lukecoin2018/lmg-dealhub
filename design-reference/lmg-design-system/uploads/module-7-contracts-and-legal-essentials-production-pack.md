### (Written lesson · ~2,300 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 7)

#### Contracts are protection, not paperwork

The negotiation lands on a yes. Money is on the table, the deliverables are agreed, you're ready to create. There's exactly one thing standing between you and starting work, and it's the thing most creators rush through or skip: get the deal in writing, properly.

A contract isn't bureaucracy. It's the document that turns the conversation you just won in Module 6 into terms you can actually enforce. It protects you from scope creep, from late payment, from a brand suddenly using your content in ways you never agreed to. And — this is the part newer creators miss — it protects the brand too. A clear contract means both sides know exactly what they're getting and what they owe. That's why a brand that resists putting things in writing, or gets defensive when you ask, is telling you something important about how the rest of the relationship will go.

Here's the good news. You don't need to write contracts from scratch, and you don't need a law degree to use one well. The Contract Builder gives you a real, lawyer-grade starting document for any of the seven common deal types, with sensible defaults and variations for everything that actually changes between partnerships. What this module gives you is the understanding underneath the tool — what each section protects, why each clause matters, and how to spot a clause that's quietly bad for you. That fluency is what lets you not just generate a contract but actually negotiate one.

One thing up front, because it matters: this module is educational, not legal advice. The Contract Builder produces a strong starting document, and for the vast majority of deals at your tier, it's everything you need. For high-value partnerships, complex co-creation arrangements, or anything involving jurisdiction-specific questions, talk to an actual attorney. The tool tells you the same thing at the bottom of every contract it generates, and it's right.

Let's go through what's in a real contract, section by section. Every contract you'll ever sign has eight standard sections — six required, two optional — and the Builder maps to each one.

#### Section 1 — Parties & Scope

This sounds like throat-clearing legal language, but it's structurally important. The Parties section establishes *who* is in this contract — and that matters because the answer changes your legal exposure.

If you operate as an individual creator, your personal name is the party. If you've formed an LLC or other business entity (more on that at the end), your business name is the party, which means the business carries the legal exposure, not you personally. The Builder asks "Individual Creator" or "Business Entity Creator" at this step for exactly this reason. Pick whichever reflects your real legal structure.

The Scope sub-section is a quick framing of what the agreement is for. It's not where you list deliverables — that's Section 2 — it's the one-line "what is this partnership about" statement that anchors everything else.

#### Section 2 — Deliverables & Timeline

This is where you protect yourself from scope creep, and it's worth getting precise. The Builder asks for the platform, the number of posts, the content type, the due date, the technical specifications, and the minimum quality requirements. It also captures the *approval process* and the *minimum days content must remain live*.

A few non-obvious things to lock down here. First, "minimum days live" prevents a brand from making you take a post down a week after publishing — they paid for visibility, you owe them visibility, but only for the period you agreed. Standard is thirty days. Second, the approval cadence matters: how many days before posting do you submit for approval, and how many business days does the brand have to respond? If they sit on your draft for two weeks, your posting deadline shouldn't be your problem. Third, the specifications protect *you* against vague "we expected something more cinematic" feedback — if you delivered to spec, you delivered.

#### Section 3 — Payment Terms

The Builder's "why this matters" line on Payment Terms is exactly right: payment terms are critical, get at least 50% upfront for new brands, Net 30 is standard but negotiate shorter if you can, late fees protect you from delayed payments. Let's unpack each.

**Upfront payment.** The Builder offers three structures: 50/50 split (recommended for most deals), 100% upfront (best for creator protection, though brands often resist), or milestone-based (good for multi-post campaigns). For any new brand, push for at least 50% upfront. It does two things — it protects your cash flow, and it proves the brand has actual budget and intent before you invest your time. A brand that won't pay any deposit is showing you something.

**Payment terms (Net days).** "Net 30" means you get paid within 30 days of invoicing. It's the industry standard, but it can strain cash flow, especially for newer creators. Net 60 or Net 90 should make you negotiate hard or decline; Net 15 or "due upon receipt" is better for you. Always know what you're agreeing to here.

**Late fees.** The Builder has this baked in at around 1.5% per month. That's standard. It's not about getting rich on late fees — it's about giving the brand a financial reason to pay on time. Brands with strict accounts-payable processes will respect it.

**Payment method.** Wire transfer and ACH are clean. PayPal works but takes a cut. Get this in writing so there are no "we don't do that" surprises.

#### Section 4 — Usage Rights & Licensing

The Builder labels this section "CRITICAL," and it's right. Usage rights are where creators leak the most value — exactly the same way they did in pricing back in Module 4. Now you lock down the language so the value you negotiated stays yours.

The Builder offers four variations: Limited 1-Year License (creator-friendly, recommended for most deals), Extended 2-Year License (longer term for established partnerships), Perpetual License (unlimited duration, requires significantly higher compensation), and Organic-Only License (no paid ads, best for maintaining authenticity).

Three rules. First, **limit the duration.** Perpetual rights mean they can use your content forever, and you should only grant that if you're being paid like you're selling them an asset — usually two to three times your normal rate. Otherwise, one year is your default. Second, **specify the platforms and the use type.** Organic posts only? Their own social channels? Their website? Paid ads? Each adds value. The Builder asks you to choose Usage Type, Permitted Platforms, Geographic Scope, and Paid Advertising Terms separately — use those distinctions to your advantage. Third, **never agree to vague "general marketing purposes."** It means anything they want.

Whitelisting — where the brand runs ads from your account — is treated as its own thing in the Rate Calculator (the +30% line). In the contract, this needs to be called out specifically if it's part of the deal. Don't let "usage rights" silently include whitelisting.

You retain ownership of your content. The brand gets a license. That's the default position, and it's the right one.

#### Section 5 — Revisions & Approvals

Two to three revision rounds is standard. Anything beyond that means you're producing multiple concepts for the price of one. The Builder lets you set this explicitly, and it's worth being clear in the document — both about how many rounds, and what counts as a revision versus new work. A caption tweak is a revision. Reshooting a Reel from scratch is new work, and it should be billed.

Also worth pinning down: the approval *turnaround*. If the brand has 72 business hours to provide feedback and they take a week, your posting deadline shouldn't slide. The contract makes that clear.

#### Section 6 — Exclusivity Terms (optional)

Exclusivity is optional in the Builder for a reason — not every deal needs it, and you should never accept it for free. If the brand wants exclusivity, you should be paid for it (we priced this in Module 4) *and* the contract has to pin down two things: the specific competitor categories, and the time period.

The Builder offers five variations on this section, reflecting how negotiable it is. Beware of broad language like "any competitor in the beauty space" — that could lock you out of half your potential deals. Push for narrow, specific definitions: "direct competitors selling refillable cleaning products" rather than "any home-care brand." And keep the period to the campaign window plus 30 days, not six months, unless the compensation justifies it.

#### Section 7 — Kill Fee & Termination (optional)

This is the section creators forget until they need it. A kill fee is what you get paid if the brand cancels the project after you've started work — and yes, this happens. Standard practice is 25–50% of the full fee if cancelled after work begins, 100% if cancelled after delivery.

Termination clauses also define how either party can end the agreement — typically with written notice for cause (breach of contract), or without cause with appropriate notice (often 30 days). The contract should also clarify what happens to content already created if the deal is terminated, and what's owed.

It's optional in the tool, but for any deal over a few hundred dollars or anything with prep work involved, include it.

#### Section 8 — FTC Compliance & Disclosures

This is non-negotiable, and the Builder makes it Required for that reason. The Federal Trade Commission requires creators in the U.S. to clearly disclose any material connection with a brand — payment, free product, affiliate commission, anything of value. Both you and the brand can face significant fines for non-compliance. (Other countries have similar rules — the UK's ASA, Canada's CCM, the EU's AVMSD-derived rules. The principle is the same: disclose clearly.)

The Builder offers four variations: Standard FTC Compliance, Brand Ambassador Disclosure (for ongoing relationships), Affiliate/Commission Disclosure, and Gifted Product Disclosure.

The rules that matter for all of them:

**Be clear and conspicuous.** Disclosures must be obvious. Burying #ad after twenty other hashtags doesn't count. On Instagram, the disclosure should be visible before the "more" button — within the first 125 characters. On video, it should be stated verbally in the first 30 seconds and shown on screen.

**Use unambiguous language.** #ad, #sponsored, or #partner are accepted. Vague terms like #collab, #sp, or "thanks [brand]" don't satisfy the requirement.

**Cover every format.** Stories need disclosure on every frame, not just the first one. Reels and TikToks need disclosure in the caption *and* verbally in the video. Gifted products still require disclosure if you're creating content about them.

**Don't rely on platform tags alone.** Instagram's "Paid Partnership" tag and YouTube's "Includes paid promotion" checkbox are useful supplements, but they don't replace explicit disclosure language. Use both.

This is the section the brand might try to soften ("just a small hashtag is fine"). Don't. The fines are on you, and your audience's trust is worth more than the brand's preference for subtler ads.

#### The business basics underneath this

Two practical notes that sit outside the contract itself but matter for the same reason — protection.

**Forming an LLC** (or your country's equivalent of a limited liability company) creates legal separation between you and your creator business. If something goes wrong — a lawsuit, a debt — your personal assets are insulated. It's not urgent at every level of income, but once you're consistently earning real partnership revenue, it's worth the few hundred dollars and a couple of hours to set one up. An accountant or a service like ZenBusiness or Incfile can do it quickly.

**Taxes.** Track every business expense — equipment, software, props, a portion of your phone and internet, professional services. These reduce your taxable income. In the U.S., set aside 25–30% of your gross income for taxes (income plus self-employment), and consider quarterly estimated payments to avoid penalties. The simplest thing you can do today: open a separate bank account for business income and expenses, so come tax season you're not untangling a year of personal-and-business mixing.

A reminder, one more time: for genuine legal or tax questions, talk to a real attorney or accountant. The Builder produces a strong document, and this module makes you fluent — neither replaces professional advice when the stakes are high.

#### Where this is going

You now understand what's in a real contract and why each section protects you. You can use the Contract Builder to produce a real, signable agreement for any deal type. And you've got the legal hygiene basics — FTC disclosure, IP retention, a business structure to grow into.

Up next is Module 8 — Delivering Results & Reporting. This is where you actually make the content the contract describes, deliver it to a professional standard, and report back to the brand in a way that gets you rebooked. The deal is locked in. Now you make it count.

### Video 7.4 — FTC Disclosure: The One You Can't Skip
**Type:** Core · **Target:** 5–6 min

We touched on FTC compliance in the contract sections, but this deserves its own deep dive — because it's the one rule that applies to *every single piece of sponsored content you make*, not just the deals with formal contracts. Get this right and you protect yourself and the brand. Get it wrong and the fines fall on you.

[On screen: "What requires disclosure"]

First, what triggers a disclosure requirement. In the U.S., the FTC says: any material connection with a brand requires clear disclosure. That includes paid sponsorships, free product, affiliate commissions, even significant discounts. If you got something of value and you're posting about it, you disclose. And it applies even to organic-feeling content — a brand sent you something and you're posting about it because you liked it? Still disclosure required. The bar is "material connection," not "I was told to post."

[On screen: "Clear and conspicuous"]

Second, the rule that matters most: clear and conspicuous. Disclosure has to be impossible to miss. Burying #ad after twenty other hashtags? That doesn't count. Hiding it at the bottom of a long caption? Doesn't count. On Instagram, the rule of thumb is *before* the "more" button — within the first 125 characters of the caption. On video, it needs to be verbally stated in the first 30 seconds *and* visible on screen.

[On screen: "The right language"]

Third, language. The accepted terms are #ad, #sponsored, and #partner. They're widely recognized and unambiguous. What doesn't satisfy the rules: #collab, #sp, "thanks [brand]," #ambassador without context. They're either too vague or too easy to miss. Stick to the explicit ones — your audience already knows what they mean, and it doesn't hurt your engagement.

[On screen: "Format-by-format"]

Fourth, the format-by-format rules. **Feed posts** — disclosure in the caption, before the "more" button. **Stories** — disclosure on *every single frame* of sponsored content, not just the first one, because viewers can land on any frame. **Reels and TikToks** — disclosure in the caption *and* spoken or shown in the video itself, since captions are sometimes minimized. **Affiliate links** — disclose even though you're only paid on conversion: "this post contains affiliate links, I may earn a commission" is fine. **Gifted products** — yes, still required. Free product is a material connection.

[On screen: "Platform tags supplement, don't replace"]

Fifth: platform tools — Instagram's "Paid Partnership" tag, YouTube's "Includes paid promotion" checkbox — are useful supplements, but they do not replace explicit disclosure. Not everyone sees them depending on how they access your content. Use them in addition to clear hashtags or verbal disclosure, not instead.

[On screen: "The contract reinforces it"]

The Contract Builder's FTC section locks all of this into the agreement so the brand can't ask you to soften it. ### Video 7.5 — Business Basics: LLC, Taxes, and Set-Asides
**Type:** Core · **Target:** 5–6 min

The contract protects you on a per-deal basis. But there's a layer underneath all your deals — your business structure — that's worth a couple of minutes, because the right setup makes everything else easier. Keep in mind: this video is educational. For your specific situation, talk to an accountant or attorney.

[On screen: "Personal liability vs. LLC"]

Start with structure. As an individual creator, you're operating as a sole proprietor by default. The business is *you* — which means your personal assets are on the hook if anything goes legally sideways. That's fine when you're starting out. But once you're consistently earning real partnership revenue — let's say $20,000 a year or more — it's worth forming an LLC, or whatever the limited liability equivalent is in your country.

An LLC creates legal separation between you and the business. Lawsuit, debt, anything — your personal assets are insulated. The cost in the U.S. is usually a few hundred dollars depending on the state, plus a small annual fee. Services like ZenBusiness, Incfile, or LegalZoom set one up in a couple of hours. Or your accountant can do it. It's not urgent at every income level, but it's one of those moves you'll be glad you made once you make it.

[On screen: "Set aside 25–30%"]

Now taxes. The single most important habit: set aside 25 to 30 percent of every payment for taxes. Why so much? Because as a self-employed creator, you owe income tax *and* self-employment tax — Social Security and Medicare in the U.S. — which adds an extra ~15% on top of regular income tax. Set aside 25 to 30 percent and you'll cover it; come tax time you're not scrambling.

The simplest mechanic: open a separate savings account, transfer the set-aside amount the same day each payment lands, don't touch it.

[On screen: "Track expenses"]

Track every business expense. Equipment — cameras, lighting, mics, computers. Software subscriptions. Props and supplies. A portion of your phone and internet bills. Professional services like an accountant. Travel for shoots or brand events. Education and courses. Even this course. These reduce your taxable income, which directly reduces your tax bill. Use QuickBooks, FreshBooks, Wave, or — if you're starting simple — a spreadsheet. Photos of receipts are fine.

And consider quarterly estimated payments. The U.S. IRS expects you to pay throughout the year, not just in April. If you expect to owe more than $1,000, you're supposed to send estimated payments four times a year. Skipping this can mean penalties even if you pay your full bill on time. Your accountant can set this up, or the IRS has a free portal.

[On screen: "Get a real accountant when it's time"]

When should you get a professional? Probably sooner than you think. If you're earning meaningfully from partnerships, an accountant familiar with self-employment will save you more than they cost. They'll find deductions you didn't know about, structure things efficiently, and remove a massive stress vector from your year. They don't have to be a "creator specialist" — a good generalist accountant handles this comfortably.
