## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,050 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 5)

#### From hoping to creating

Everything you've built so far has been pointing at this moment. You have your numbers from Module 1, a target list and a media kit from Module 2, and — after Module 4 — a rate you can defend. Now you go get the deals. Proactive pitching is what flips you from a creator hoping to be discovered into one who actively creates opportunities, on your terms, with the brands you actually want.

But a pitch isn't a shot in the dark, and it isn't "I love your brand, let's collab." A pitch that works is a small, deliberate piece of writing with a job: to make a busy brand contact stop, recognize that you understand them, and want to reply. The good news is that this is a learnable structure, not a talent. In this module you'll learn the exact anatomy of a pitch that gets read, when to send it, and how to follow up so persistence reads as professionalism rather than pestering. Let's build the email first.

#### The anatomy of a pitch that gets a reply

A strong pitch has six parts, and each one earns the right to the next.

**The subject line** is your first and often only chance to get opened. Generic lines like "Collaboration Opportunity" or "Partnership Inquiry" get ignored — they're indistinguishable from the dozens of other pitches in the inbox. Make yours specific and value-focused. "Partnership Proposal: Reaching 50,000 Engaged Home-Design Enthusiasts" tells the recipient exactly who you reach and implies the value. "Collaboration Idea: [Brand] + Sustainable Fashion Creator" creates curiosity while staying specific. The subject line should make the value obvious before the email is even opened.

**The opening** — your first two or three sentences — has one job: prove this isn't a mass email. Reference something specific and recent about the brand: a product launch, a campaign you noticed, a post that resonated, a value you share. Something like, "I was excited to see your recent launch of the EcoClean line — the focus on biodegradable ingredients lines up exactly with what my audience and I care about." That single sentence signals you did your homework, which is exactly the research you gathered back in Module 2.

**The value proposition** is one tight paragraph that says who you are and why you're worth their attention. Here's where your Module 1 numbers do the work — but choose them strategically. Don't just state a follower count; lead with the metrics that prove influence. "I'm a sustainable-living creator helping millennials cut their environmental impact. My audience of 50,000 maintains a 6.8% engagement rate — well above average — and 82% are women aged 25–40 actively shopping for eco-friendly alternatives." That tells a brand their exact customer is here and paying attention.

**The alignment paragraph** makes the strategic case, and it goes beyond "I love your products." Show the specific overlap between your audience's needs and what the brand sells, ideally with a concrete detail. "My audience constantly asks for eco-friendly cleaning recommendations — in a recent poll, 73% said they're actively trying to switch but can't find brands they trust." Now the brand sees an audience already primed for them, where a partnership would feel like a natural recommendation rather than an ad.

**The content ideas** are where you make it easy to say yes. Offer two or three concrete concepts that show how you'd integrate the product naturally. They don't need to be fully built campaigns, just specific enough to prove you understand both the brand and your audience — "a week-long swap series replacing my usual products with theirs," "a side-by-side comparison post," "a Reel showing unexpected uses." Specifics make the partnership feel real and imminent.

**The call to action** should be clear but not pushy: suggest one concrete next step. "I'd love to find fifteen minutes to talk through how we might work together — are you free for a quick call next week?" gives them an easy action. Mention that your media kit is attached — the one you built in Module 2 — so they have everything without having to ask. Then close with a professional signature: full name, handles with follower counts, phone, and email, so reaching you is frictionless.

A note on DMs: for warmer relationships or smaller brands — especially the ones you've been engaging with since Module 2 — a DM can work better than email. Same principles, just shorter and more casual: a specific opener, one line of value, one clear ask. Your templates pack includes DM versions for exactly this.

#### Timing: pitch when the budget is being decided

The best pitch sent at the wrong time still loses. Brands plan their marketing calendars six to twelve weeks ahead, sometimes more — so pitch too early and you're forgotten, too late and the budget's already spent. The sweet spot is roughly eight to ten weeks before a campaign would naturally run.

Seasonal campaigns reward planning backward from the date. For holiday partnerships, pitch in August or September — waiting until November means you've missed it. Back-to-school is planned in June and July, summer campaigns in January and February, and Mother's Day or Father's Day in February and March. Put these windows in your calendar with reminders so you're reaching out at the right moment, not scrambling after.

Don't assume any time of year is off-limits, though. Plenty of brands keep year-round budgets for ongoing awareness, launches, and a constant need for content — these evergreen opportunities respond to a good pitch whenever it lands. And even when current-quarter budgets are locked, brands are already planning the next one: a Q4 budget closed in September doesn't stop them planning Q1, which makes fall an excellent time to pitch for winter and spring. Finally, watch product launch cycles in your niche — brands launching something new actively need creators to build buzz. Follow your target brands closely and sign up for their newsletters so you catch launches early and can pitch right around them.

#### Following up without being annoying

Most pitches that turn into deals do so after a follow-up — yours wasn't rejected, it was missed in an inbox getting hundreds of emails a week. The skill is persistence that reads as professional.

Wait five to seven business days after your first email, then send a brief follow-up that references your original message and includes it in the thread so they don't have to dig. Keep it light: a couple of friendly sentences restating your interest and making it easy to reply. If that gets no response, wait another five to seven days and send one final, even shorter note that acknowledges they're busy and leaves the door open — "totally understand if the timing isn't right; I'd love to stay on your radar for the future."

After two follow-ups with no reply, stop. Continuing past that point works against you. A non-response usually just means no budget right now, or they're already working with someone — none of it personal, and all of it can change. Add the brand back to your list and try again in six months to a year, because the brand that ignores you today can become an enthusiastic partner later.

This is exactly what the Outreach Tracker from Module 2 is for: log every pitch, the contact, the dates you followed up, and the outcome. It keeps you from accidentally re-pitching the same person too soon, shows you which approaches and timing actually work, and lets you nurture these relationships over the long haul instead of treating each pitch as a one-off.

#### Where this is going

You now have the full pitching system: a six-part email that gets read, the timing to land it when budgets are live, and a follow-up cadence that's persistent without being pushy. Send enough of these and you'll start getting the reply every creator wants — "let's set up a call."

And that call is exactly where Module 6 begins. Because the moment a brand says "let's talk," you've moved from pitching to negotiating — and that's where knowing your worth turns into getting paid it. Pick a real brand from your list, build your pitch, and send it. Then bring the conversation it starts into the next module.

### Video 5.4 — Before & After: A Pitch Teardown
**Type:** Teardown · **Target:** 6–7 min

Let's make this concrete. I'm going to show you a real-world "before" pitch — the kind that gets ignored — and rebuild it into an "after" using our six-part structure. Watch what changes.

[On screen: "The Before"]

Here's the before. Subject line: "Collaboration Opportunity." Body: "Hi, I'm a content creator with 40,000 followers and I love your brand! I think we'd be a great fit and I'd love to collaborate. Let me know if you're interested! Thanks!"

Let's diagnose it. The subject line is invisible — it's identical to every other pitch. The opening says nothing specific about *this* brand; swap the name and it could go to anyone. The only stat is a raw follower count, which tells them nothing about whether your audience is their customer. There's no reason given for why this partnership makes sense, no idea of what you'd actually make, and the ask — "let me know if you're interested" — makes *them* do all the work. This email is all about the creator and zero about the brand. Delete.

[On screen: "The After"]

Now the after. Same creator, same 40,000 followers. Subject line: "Partnership Idea: Bringing [Brand]'s Refillable Line to 40K Low-Waste Home Creators." Already we know who they reach and the value.

Opening: "I've been following [Brand] since your refillable cleaner launch last month — the no-plastic refill system is exactly the kind of swap my audience asks me about constantly." Specific, recent, researched.

Value prop: "I create low-waste home content for 40,000 followers at a 7% engagement rate, and 80% of them are women 28 to 42 who are actively trying to cut household plastic." That's their customer, described precisely.

Alignment: "In a poll last month, 68% of my audience said they want to switch to refillable products but don't know which brands to trust." There's the open door.

Content ideas: "I'd love to do a 30-day refill challenge, a 'what's under my sink' swap reveal, and a Reel debunking refill myths." Concrete and ready.

CTA: "Could we grab fifteen minutes next week to talk it through? I've attached my media kit with full audience data." Easy yes, everything provided.

[On screen: "Same creator. Different outcome."]

Same person, same following — completely different email. The after isn't longer for the sake of it; every line does a job, and every job is about the *brand*, not the creator. That's the whole difference between ignored and "let's set up a call." Your turn — build yours in the workbook.
