## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,000 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 2)

#### Stop waiting to be discovered

In Module 1 you got your numbers — your engagement rate, your audience snapshot, an honest read on where your partnership business stands. Now we put them to work, and the first job is the one most creators skip: building a system that brings the right brands to you instead of waiting and hoping.

Here's the trap almost everyone falls into, no matter their size. They're reactive. They wait for a DM. They take whatever inbound happens to land. They have no real say in which brands they work with — they just react to whoever shows up. A 300,000-follower creator can be every bit as reactive as a 15,000-follower one; the only difference is the size of the inbox. Being reactive means your income depends on luck and other people's timing, and it means you're often working with brands you wouldn't have chosen.

Going proactive flips that. Instead of waiting, you generate a steady flow of opportunities from the brands *you* actually want. And the way you do that is by running three channels at once.

#### The three channels opportunities travel through

There are exactly three ways a brand opportunity reaches you. **You go to them** — direct outreach. **A platform connects you** — marketplaces and networks. **They come to you** — inbound discovery. Most creators lean on one and ignore the rest. The ones who build reliable partnership income run all three in parallel, so that when one is quiet, the others keep producing. Let's build each.

#### Channel 1 — Direct outreach: your target-brand list

Direct outreach puts you in the driver's seat. It's how you land collaborations with specific brands you're genuinely excited about, rather than whoever happens to find you.

Start by building a list of twenty to thirty target brands. The criteria: brands you already use and love, brands whose aesthetic matches your content, and brands whose customers look like your audience. A fast way to find good candidates is to look at what your peers and similar creators are already partnering with — those companies clearly invest in creator marketing and understand the value. And don't limit yourself to the obvious giants. Some of the best partnerships come from smaller or newer brands that don't yet have a mature influencer program but would benefit enormously from one — those relationships are often easier to start and grow into something long-term.

Then research each brand before you ever reach out: their recent campaigns, their latest launches, and where partnership decisions seem to be made. You're looking for specifics you can reference later, because specifics are what separate a real pitch from a generic one.

Finally — and this is the move most creators miss — warm the relationship up before you pitch. Spend time genuinely engaging with the brand on social: thoughtful comments, sharing their content when it actually resonates, tagging them when you really do use their product. This isn't a trick; it's building authentic familiarity. By the time your outreach lands, you're not a cold stranger — you're a name they've seen and a creator who clearly already likes them. (We'll write the actual pitch in Module 5. Right now we're building the list and the relationships that make that pitch land.)

#### Your media kit: the asset that does the selling

Before you go to market, you need the one document that does your selling for you: your media kit. This is where the numbers you captured in Module 1 finally go to work.

A media kit is a single, visually clean page that lets a brand understand your value in thirty seconds. It includes a short bio that names your niche and what makes you valuable, your audience demographics with real numbers and percentages, your engagement metrics — that pricing-power number from Module 1 — a few examples of your best content, brief case studies if you have any past results to show, and your contact details and handles. Brands often forward media kits to colleagues, so it has to stand on its own without you there to explain it.

For micro and mid-tier creators specifically, here's the strategic point: lead with engagement and audience fit, not raw follower count. A media kit that opens with "6.8% engagement rate, audience 82% women aged 25–40 actively shopping for sustainable products" is far more persuasive than one that just leads with a follower number. That framing is your edge — use it.

Update your media kit every quarter so it always reflects your current metrics and your best recent work.

#### Channel 2 — Platforms and networks: your matchmakers

The second channel does some of the work for you. Influencer marketing platforms exist to connect creators and brands, and they handle a lot of the administrative load — contracts, content approval, payments. You build a profile, then either apply to campaigns brands have posted or get discovered by brands searching for creators who match certain criteria. Platforms like AspireIQ, CreatorIQ, Grin, and TRIBE operate in this space, and some specialize by niche — LTK (formerly rewardStyle) dominates fashion and lifestyle on an affiliate model, while others like Hashtag Paid focus on campaigns designed to feel authentic.

Affiliate networks are a related lane worth setting up in parallel. Networks such as ShareASale, Rakuten, Amazon Associates, Impact, and CJ Affiliate give you access to thousands of commission-based programs. The per-sale payout is smaller than a flat fee, but affiliate income is passive and compounds over time, especially on evergreen content — so it's a smart background layer under everything else you do.

Talent agencies are a later-stage option. Good ones take a commission, usually fifteen to twenty percent, in exchange for connecting you with premium opportunities, negotiating on your behalf, and handling contracts. But they're selective and generally only sign creators with proven track records — so don't rush to chase representation. Build your foundation first; the best agencies tend to come to you once you've demonstrated consistent success.

One caveat that keeps this evergreen: platforms and programs change. Treat the names above as a starting map, not gospel — research which platforms are actually most active in *your* niche right now, and put your energy into a strong profile on those.

#### Channel 3 — Inbound discovery: be findable and bookable

The third channel is about making it effortless for brands to find you and contact you when they're already searching. Think of it as your inbound magnet.

Start with your profiles. Put a professional business email directly in your bio — not a personal address, and not a contact form that makes brands jump through hoops. Use a Business or Professional account designation where your platform offers one, since that signals you're open to collaborations. And make your openness explicit with a line like "Partnership inquiries:" right in the bio so there's zero ambiguity.

Use consistent industry and niche hashtags so brands researching potential partners actually surface your content. Balance matters here — your hashtag strategy should serve your audience first, with brand discoverability as a welcome side effect. And don't put #ad or #sponsored on content that isn't sponsored; it creates confusion and can hurt you.

Build a simple landing page. It doesn't need to be elaborate — a single page with your media kit, a portfolio of your best work, a short bio, and a contact form is plenty. Tools like Squarespace, Wix, or Carrd get you there without technical skills. Having one signals that you treat this as a real business, and it gives brands a single place to learn everything they need. Link it everywhere — every bio, every email signature, your media kit.

Finally, invest in your creator network. Brands constantly ask the creators they already work with whether they know others who'd be a good fit. If you're a genuine, generous part of a creator community, you'll be the name that comes to mind when those referrals get asked for. Collaborations also expose you to other creators' audiences, which brings its own opportunities. Approach this as abundance, not competition — there's far more work to go around than any one creator can take, and the people who lift others up are the ones who get lifted in return.

#### Where this is going

You now have a pipeline engine, not a waiting game: a researched target list, a media kit that sells for you, platforms and networks working in the background, and an inbound setup that makes you easy to find and book. That flow of opportunity is the thing that ends the feast-or-famine cycle.

But more opportunities mean more decisions — and not every brand that comes your way deserves a yes. In Module 3 we build your filter: how to vet an opportunity so a single paycheck never costs you the audience trust you've spent years earning. After that, Module 4 sets your rates, and Modules 5 and 6 turn this pipeline into pitches that get replies and negotiations you win. Bring your target list and your media kit forward — they're about to become the raw material for everything that follows.
