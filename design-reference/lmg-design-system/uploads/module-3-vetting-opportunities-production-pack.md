# Module 3 — Vetting Opportunities
### Production Pack: Module Guide + Video Scripts

**Course:** The Complete Brand Partnership Playbook
**Audience:** Micro to mid-tier creators (~10K–500K)
**Module job:** Give the creator a repeatable filter for deciding which opportunities to pursue. By the end they can run any deal through a scorecard, know the questions to ask a brand before committing, and judge true audience fit — so a paycheck never costs them the trust that makes them valuable.

**Framing rule (carry forward):** Vetting is strategic, not just protective. Two outcomes to keep front-and-center: (1) your audience's trust is your most valuable asset, worth more than any single payment; (2) aligned partnerships perform better, which makes you more bookable and better-paid. Saying no to the wrong deals is how you earn the right to charge premium rates for the right ones.

**Scope note:** Module 3 is about *fit and decision* — is this deal right for me and my audience? Scam/payment red flags, defensive reputation tactics, and the "decline gracefully" script live in Module 10. The brand-questions here (usage rights, compensation, exclusivity, deliverables) are framed as information-gathering to make the decision; pricing, negotiating, and contracting them comes in Modules 4, 6, and 7.

## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,000 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 3)

#### Why "yes to everything" quietly kills your business

In Module 2 you built a pipeline. As it starts producing, you'll face a new problem — a good one: more opportunities than you can or should take. And here's where a lot of creators sabotage themselves. They say yes to everything, because every deal looks like money and turning down money feels wrong.

It isn't, and here's why. Your single most valuable asset is not your follower count or even your content — it's your audience's trust. That trust is what makes a brand willing to pay you in the first place, because it's what turns your recommendation into their sales. And trust is fragile. It takes years to build and can be damaged in a moment by one partnership that doesn't fit — a product your audience knows you'd never actually use, a brand whose values clash with everything you've stood for. One well-paid but misaligned deal can quietly erode the thing your entire business is built on.

But I want to be clear that vetting isn't only defensive. There's an offensive reason to be selective, and it's the one that grows your income: aligned partnerships simply perform better. When you promote something your audience genuinely wants, the content converts, the brand sees results, and you get rebooked at higher rates and referred to other brands. Misaligned deals do the opposite — they flop, the brand blames the creator, and that becomes part of your track record. So being selective isn't leaving money on the table. It's how you make more of it. The creators who are known for being discerning have audiences who trust their recommendations more — which is exactly why those creators can charge a premium.

This module gives you the filter: questions to ask yourself, questions to ask the brand, and a way to judge real audience fit. Let's start with you.

#### Five questions to ask yourself

Before you even reply to a brand, run the opportunity through five questions about *you* and your audience.

**Does this brand align with my values and content style?** Your audience follows you for a reason — your perspective, your taste, your judgment. A partnership that contradicts those things creates a jarring disconnect they'll notice immediately. The simplest test: would your audience be surprised to see you promoting this? If you've built a brand around natural, sustainable living and you suddenly promote fast fashion, that surprise is a red flag. If it feels like something you'd naturally recommend anyway, that's a green light.

**Would my audience genuinely benefit from or be interested in this?** Partnership content performs best when it's actually useful — when it solves a problem your audience has, speaks to their interests, or improves their lives. If the product is irrelevant to them and you're only promoting it for the check, the content will underperform and your audience will feel it. They're sophisticated; they can tell the difference between genuine enthusiasm and a paycheck.

**Can I authentically recommend this product?** This one goes beyond niche fit to the product itself. Ideally you already use and love it. At minimum, you should be willing to test it thoroughly before you create anything. If the quality doesn't meet your standards, if you wouldn't spend your own money on it, or if you have real doubts about whether it works — decline, no matter what they're paying. Your recommendations are your currency. Spend them only on things you'd stand behind for free.

**Does this fit my long-term brand strategy?** Every partnership you accept becomes part of your portfolio and shapes how both brands and audiences see you. Take deals purely for the money and you can blur your own identity, making it harder to attract the partnerships you actually want. If you're building toward a premium, aspirational brand, a stack of budget deals works against you — and vice versa. Ask where you want to be in three years, and whether this deal moves you toward it or away from it.

**Am I comfortable being publicly associated with this company — not just its product?** When you promote a brand, you're implicitly endorsing the company behind it, and audiences increasingly hold creators accountable for that fuller picture. So do a quick check on the brand's reputation and recent news before you commit; a simple search for the company name plus "controversy" surfaces most of what you'd want to know. If something there would make you uncomfortable defending the partnership to your audience, take that seriously. (We go deeper on protecting your reputation and spotting genuine bad actors in Module 10 — for now, this is a gut-check.)

#### The questions to ask the brand

The five questions above are about whether you *want* the deal. The next set surfaces whether it's actually a *good* deal — and you ask these before you commit, not after.

Ask about their **campaign goals and how they'll measure success.** Are they after awareness, traffic, or conversions, and what metrics define a win? This tells you what to create, and it also flags unrealistic expectations early — if they're expecting a viral hit or guaranteed sales numbers, that's a conversation to have now, not a disappointment to discover later.

Ask about **creative freedom.** How much input will you have? The best partnerships let you apply your expertise about what resonates with your audience while hitting the brand's key messages. Be wary if they want to script every word and approve every detail — that usually produces stiff content that performs poorly.

Ask about the **timeline** — when they need concepts, how long their review takes, when content posts, and how many revision rounds are included — so you can judge whether it fits your capacity honestly.

Then ask about three things that directly affect what this deal is worth: **usage rights** (where and how long they'll use your content, and whether in paid ads), **compensation structure** (amount, schedule, and method — when exactly do you get paid), and **exclusivity** (will you be barred from competitors, for how long, and how broadly defined). You don't need to negotiate these yet — we price them in Module 4, negotiate them in Module 6, and lock them down in Module 7. Right now you're just gathering the facts you need to decide.

Finally, get the **deliverables** spelled out exactly — how many posts, on which platforms, in what formats, plus any events or extra activities. This single step prevents the scope creep that turns a fair deal into an unfair one.

#### Judging real audience fit

The last piece of vetting is matching the brand's target customer against your actual audience — and this is where your Module 1 snapshot pays off again.

Start with the obvious layer: demographics. Pull your audience data — age, location, gender, interests — and compare it against who the brand is trying to reach. A mismatch here doesn't automatically kill the deal, but it does mean you should have an honest conversation with the brand about whether the partnership makes strategic sense for them, and adjust expectations accordingly.

But don't stop at demographics, because the deeper fit is psychographic — your audience's attitudes, values, and lifestyle. A brand might match your audience perfectly on age and location and still be wrong for them. If your audience values minimalism and the brand is built on excess consumption, the numbers line up but the alignment doesn't. So think about the actual conversations you have in your comments and DMs: What matters to these people? What problems are they trying to solve? What are they already interested in buying? A partnership that speaks to your audience's real needs and values will always outperform one that's merely demographically aligned — and "it'll outperform" is exactly what makes you worth more to the brand.

#### Where this is going

You now have a filter: five questions about whether you want the deal, a set of questions that reveal whether it's a good one, and a way to judge real audience fit. Run every opportunity through it, and you'll protect your trust *and* raise your hit rate — which is the combination that lets you charge more.

And charging more is exactly where we go next. In Module 4 we set your rates — properly, with confidence, and including all the things creators routinely give away free. The deals that clear your filter are the ones worth pricing well, so bring the opportunities you're currently weighing. We're about to put real numbers on them.

### Video 3.3 — Audience Fit: Demographics vs. Psychographics
**Type:** Core · **Target:** 5–6 min

The final piece of vetting is the one creators most often get wrong: whether the brand's target customer actually matches your audience. And there are two layers to this — and the second one is where the real money is.

[On screen: "Layer 1: Demographics"]

Layer one is demographics — the obvious stuff. Pull your audience data from Module 1: age, location, gender, interests. Compare it against who the brand is trying to reach. Now, a mismatch here doesn't automatically kill the deal — but it does mean you owe the brand an honest conversation about whether this makes strategic sense for them, and you adjust expectations together. Better to flag it upfront than to deliver a campaign to the wrong audience and have it flop.

[On screen: "Layer 2: Psychographics"]

But here's the layer most people skip — psychographics. That's your audience's attitudes, values, and lifestyle. And this is where a deal can look perfect on paper and still be wrong. Picture a brand that matches your audience exactly on age and location — but your audience values minimalism, and this brand is all about buying more, more, more. The demographics line up. The alignment doesn't. And your audience will feel that mismatch even if they can't name it.

[On screen: "Listen to your comments & DMs"]

So how do you actually read your audience's psychographics? You already have the data — it's in your comments and your DMs. What do these people care about? What problems are they trying to solve? What are they already asking you to recommend? That's the real picture of who your audience is. And a partnership that speaks to those actual needs and values will always outperform one that just matches on age and zip code.

[On screen: "Performance = your leverage"]

And don't lose sight of why this matters for you specifically: a deal that fits psychographically performs better, and performance is your leverage. It's what gets you rebooked, referred, and paid more. So this isn't just about protecting your audience — it's about picking the deals that make you look great.

[On screen: workbook prompt]

In your workbook there's an audience-fit check — a quick side-by-side of the brand's target against your actual audience, on both layers. Run it on any deal you're seriously considering.

### Video 3.4 — "Should I Take This Deal?" — Three Walkthroughs
**Type:** Teardown · **Target:** 6–7 min

Let's make this real. I'm going to run three opportunities through the scorecard from your workbook — scoring each on values fit, authentic recommendation, audience benefit, long-term fit, company comfort, and audience match, one to five. Watch how the decision falls out. Remember the rule: the first two — values and authentic recommendation — are deal-breakers. Score low there and it's a no, no matter the total.

[On screen: "Deal 1 — the obvious yes"]

Deal one. You're a home-cooking creator, and a quality cookware brand you already own and use reaches out. Values fit — five, this is exactly your lane. Authentic recommendation — five, you literally cook with their pans every day. Audience benefit — five, your followers constantly ask what cookware you use. Long-term fit — five. Company comfort — you search them, nothing concerning, four. Audience match — five, your people are home cooks, their customer is home cooks. That's a 29 out of 30. This is the kind of deal you say yes to fast and price well. Easy.

[On screen: "Deal 2 — the tempting trap"]

Deal two. Same creator. A trendy detox-tea brand offers you double your usual rate. The money is genuinely tempting. But: values fit — two; you've never touched detox teas and your audience knows it. Authentic recommendation — one; you don't use it, you have doubts it even works. Right there, two deal-breakers scoring one and two — this is a no, and we don't even need the total. And notice *why* it's a no: take it, and your audience clocks the disconnect, the content flops, the brand's unhappy, and you've spent trust you can't easily earn back — all for one check. The double rate is a trap. Pass.

[On screen: "Deal 3 — the 'maybe,' done right"]

Deal three is the interesting one — the maybe. A meal-kit company reaches out. Values fit — four, food's your world. Authentic recommendation — three; you haven't tried this specific service, but you're willing to test it. Audience benefit — three; some of your audience cooks from scratch and might see meal kits as the opposite of what they follow you for. Long-term fit — four. Company comfort — four. Audience match — three; demographically yes, but psychographically it's a question mark. That lands around 21 — squarely in "proceed, but have a conversation."

So what do you do with a 21? You don't decline, and you don't blindly accept. You test the product first to move that authenticity score, and you talk to the brand about an angle that fits your audience — maybe "meal kits for the nights you can't cook from scratch" instead of replacing scratch cooking entirely. You turn a maybe into a yes by fixing the gaps, or you walk if you can't.

[On screen: "The scorecard gives you a reason"]

That's the whole point of the scorecard. It turns a gut feeling into a clear reason — so you can say yes with confidence, no without guilt, or "let's adjust" with a specific plan. Run your real opportunities through it next.
