## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,150 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 8)

#### Delivery is where deals compound

You've found the brand, vetted them, priced the deal, pitched it, negotiated it, and locked it down with a contract. Now comes the part of the business that quietly does more for your future income than any of the steps before it: actually delivering the work, and reporting on what it did.

Here's why this module is more important than it looks. Pricing decides what you earn on *this* deal. Delivery and reporting decide what you earn on the *next ten*. A creator who consistently delivers content that performs — and who tells the brand what it did in a clear, professional report — gets rebooked, gets referred, and can raise their rate the next time without resistance. A creator who delivers something average and goes silent after posting doesn't.

The flip side matters too. A bad delivery — late, off-brief, low-quality, or worst of all, missing the FTC disclosure — can torch a relationship and end any chance of a retainer. So this module is about two things: making the content the contract describes, to a standard the brand will brag about, and reporting on it in a way that proves your value beyond the post itself. Let's start before the camera even comes out.

#### Pre-production: plan before you shoot

The biggest difference between content that lands and content that wastes a day is whether you planned it. Most creators try to plan in their head while filming. That's how you finish a shoot, sit down to edit, and realize you forgot the close-up of the product label that the brand explicitly asked for. Don't be that creator.

Two documents handle this — and you only ever need these two — your content brief and your shot list.

**Your content brief** is the short internal document that captures everything that has to be true about the content before you start. It's roughly a page. Key message — what's the one thing the brand wants the audience to take away? The brand's unique value proposition — what makes their product different, in their words? Required elements — specific shots, hashtags, talking points, the call-to-action they want, the exact disclosure language. Creative angle — your storytelling approach, so the content feels like *you* and not like an ad. Emotional tone — what the audience should feel. And the FTC disclosure plan, written out — because forgetting this is the single most expensive mistake a creator can make.

You build the brief from the contract you signed in Module 7 plus whatever creative direction the brand sent. If anything in the brief is unclear, ask the brand before you shoot, not after. Brands respect creators who clarify upfront and resent ones who deliver off-brief and blame the brief.

**Your shot list** is the practical companion — the specific shots or scenes you need to capture, in order. Wide shots for context, close-ups for product detail, action shots showing use, the "hero" beauty shot, and any behind-the-scenes moments that add authenticity. Building a shot list before the shoot prevents the most common loss in creator content: finishing the day and realizing you missed the one essential frame that means everything has to be reshot.

Both documents take ten to twenty minutes to fill in. They save you hours.

A note on **drafting your caption ahead of time.** Most creators write the caption in the five minutes before posting. That's where rushed disclosure errors happen, where the call-to-action gets dropped, where the brand's key message gets lost. Draft your caption when you write the brief — when you have time to refine it, ensure the disclosure is in the first 125 characters, work in the CTA naturally, and read it back the next day to catch anything weird. Then on posting day, all you do is post.

#### Production quality: what brand-ready actually looks like

You don't need expensive gear, and you don't need to be a filmmaker. You need work that looks intentional and consistent. Brands hire you for your creative perspective and your audience — but the content has to clear a professional bar so it reflects well on their product.

Four areas matter, in order of impact.

**Lighting** is the single biggest visible difference between amateur and professional content, full stop. Natural light is the gold standard — shoot during golden hour outdoors, or near a large window indoors. When natural light isn't an option, a basic ring light for talking-head shots or a small LED panel for fill is a real investment that pays back across every deal. Strategic desk lamps with diffusion work in a pinch. What doesn't work: your phone's built-in flash, or a single overhead light directly above your face.

**Audio** is where most creators lose viewers without realizing it. Bad audio makes even a beautifully shot video feel amateur. Your phone or camera's built-in mic is fine in a quiet room, marginal anywhere else. A clip-on lavalier mic for talking-head and voiceover, or a shotgun mic on your camera for vlog-style content, is a small purchase that transforms how your content reads. Test your audio before you commit to a full shoot; finding out you had a hum when you sit down to edit means reshooting.

**Composition and framing** separate intentional content from snapshot-y content. Rule of thirds — put your subject and any horizon line along the gridlines, not dead-center. Pay attention to your background; clutter is the fastest way to make a shot look unconsidered. Vary your angles across a piece of content — a wide for context, a medium, a close-up — so the visual rhythm holds attention.

**Brand integration** is the one that distinguishes a sponsored post that lands from one that gets scrolled past. The amateur move is awkwardly holding the product to camera with a stiff smile. The professional move is integrating the product naturally into your existing content style — using it in your real morning routine, styling it into an outfit you'd actually wear, reviewing it in your usual format. Your audience can sniff out an ad from the first second; the content that works makes them think "that looks useful" before they realize it's sponsored.

#### Optimization: the first hour after posting matters

Posting strategically is the part most creators skip entirely. They finish the content, hit publish, and walk away. Don't. The first sixty to ninety minutes after posting set the trajectory of a post — early engagement signals to the algorithm that the content is worth showing to more people.

Three habits make a meaningful difference.

**Post when your audience is actually online.** Most platforms show you exactly when your followers are most active. Schedule for those windows. For many creators, weekday mornings (7–9 AM) and early afternoons (1–3 PM) work well, but check your own data because audiences vary widely. Posting at a high-activity window is one of the easiest wins available.

**Engage in the first hour.** When followers comment, reply within minutes — with thoughtful replies that continue the conversation, not just "thanks!" This signals to the algorithm that you're driving engagement, and it makes your followers feel seen. Set aside time right after posting to monitor and respond; don't post and disappear.

**Cross-promote across your formats.** If you posted a feed piece, drop a Story pointing at it. Use interactive stickers — polls, questions, quizzes — to boost engagement on the Story promoting your sponsored post. This is a free amplification layer most creators ignore.

And for partnerships with **clickable links** — Story link stickers, TikTok bio links, affiliate codes — minimize friction. If you're sending traffic to a product page, make sure the exact product you featured is easy to find from the landing page. If there's a discount code, remind people of it and how to use it. Every step of friction lowers your conversion rate, which lowers the campaign's results, which lowers what you can charge next time. This stuff compounds.

#### Reporting: how to get rebooked

Here's the move that separates creators who get one-off deals from creators who build retainers: **send a performance report without being asked.**

Most creators wait for the brand to follow up. Most brands never do — they're busy, the campaign moved on, your post is now their marketing team's last quarter. So the brand never sees the impact, never has the data to justify a follow-up, and you become a vendor instead of a partner.

Don't be a vendor. Within 48 to 72 hours of the agreed reporting window closing, send the brand a clean, short performance report. It does three things in one move: it demonstrates professionalism, it gives them the data to justify booking you again, and it positions you as a strategic partner rather than just a content creator.

A good report has four parts.

**The headline.** A single summary statement at the top with the strongest result. "The Reel reached 47,000 impressions at 7.2% engagement, both above projected benchmarks." That's all the brand's manager needs to forward this email up.

**The metrics.** Screenshots of platform insights are fine — you don't need a polished slide deck. Include impressions, reach, engagement rate (with the formula or platform calculation), specific engagement numbers (likes, comments, saves, shares — saves and shares are particularly strong indicators), and any link clicks or coupon-code uses if applicable.

**The context.** This is the part that separates a vendor's data dump from a partner's report. Compare the campaign's performance to your typical content. "The sponsored Reel hit 7.2% engagement against my usual 6.0% — outperformed my baseline." If something underperformed, address it honestly: "Saves were lower than my average, likely because the call-to-action pushed for site visits rather than saves. Worth testing a save-first CTA next time." Brands value honest analysis more than they value hiding bad news.

**Qualitative feedback.** Share interesting comments, questions you got from your audience, themes you noticed. This is market research the brand can't get anywhere else, and it's where you start sounding like a strategist. "Several followers asked where the refillable cleaner is stocked in the UK — might be worth a regional landing page." Now you're useful beyond the post.

End the report with a soft prompt for next steps: "Happy to share full insights on a call, and to talk about what we could do next." That's the line that turns one deal into a conversation about more deals — which is exactly what Module 9 builds into a retainer.

#### Where this is going

You can now plan content that won't waste a day, produce it to a standard brands brag about, optimize its first hour for maximum performance, and report on it like a strategic partner instead of a vendor. That last shift — partner instead of vendor — is the doorway to everything in Module 9, where we take a single successful campaign and turn it into a recurring monthly retainer. Bring your performance report from a real campaign with you. We're about to use it to build something much bigger.
