## PART 1 — MODULE GUIDE
### (Written lesson · ~2,150 words · lifts directly into the ebook as Chapter 6)

#### Where knowing your worth becomes getting paid

In Module 4 you figured out what you're worth. This is the module where that number stops being a private calculation and becomes money in your account. A brand replied to your pitch, you got on a call or into an email thread, and now there's a number on the table — theirs or yours. What happens next is negotiation, and it's where confident creators quietly separate themselves from the ones who accept whatever they're offered.

Here's the reframe that changes everything: every part of a partnership is negotiable. Not just the fee — the deliverables, the timeline, the usage rights, the exclusivity, the payment terms, all of it. Most creators don't realize this, so they treat a brand's first offer as a fixed reality to accept or reject. It isn't. It's an opening position. And the brands that become your best long-term partners actually respect creators who negotiate professionally, because it signals you treat this as a business — which makes you someone worth working with again.

This module gives you three things: how to walk in prepared so you're negotiating from strength, the specific tactics that move a deal in your favor, and the boundaries and warning signs that tell you when to hold firm or walk away. And you have a tool in your corner — the Negotiation Assistant — that drafts professional responses for whatever stage you're at. But just like with pricing, we teach the logic first, because on a live call there's no time to open an app. You need to think on your feet, and the tool is there for when you want to craft the perfect written reply. Let's start with preparation, because most negotiations are won before they begin.

#### Know your worth before you say a word

The single biggest predictor of how a negotiation goes is how prepared you walked in. Three things to have locked down first.

**Your market context.** Know roughly what creators with your stats, niche, and engagement are charging. Creator communities and groups where people share real rate information are gold here — they give you benchmark data so a brand can't exploit the fact that you're guessing. The more you know what's normal, the harder you are to lowball.

**Your performance proof.** This is your leverage made concrete. Can you show that your audience takes action — that past content drove clicks, sales, or real engagement? "My last three sponsored posts averaged 9% engagement and drove around 500 clicks to the brand's site" is a fundamentally stronger position than hoping they see your potential. Bring receipts.

**Your floor.** This is the number you set back in Module 4 — your absolute minimum, accounting for your time, your opportunity cost, and the value of your platform. Below it, the deal doesn't make business sense. Knowing your floor cold is what gives you the nerve to politely walk away, and that willingness to walk is, paradoxically, your greatest source of power. Desperation weakens you; a known walk-away number strengthens you. Brands can feel the difference.

#### The tactics that move a deal

With preparation done, here are the tactics that actually shift outcomes in your favor.

**Anchor above your target.** Open your rate quote about fifteen to twenty percent above what you actually want. This is standard professional practice, not greed — it gives you room to make concessions that feel meaningful to the brand while still landing at your real target. Open at your bottom line and you've got nowhere to go but down. (This is the slight-premium opening we flagged in Module 4.)

**Trade variables instead of cutting price.** When a brand says "that's over our budget" — and they often will — resist the reflex to drop your rate. Instead, change what they get for the money. Can you reduce the number of deliverables? Shorten the usage-rights window? Drop the exclusivity? Extend the timeline to a less busy period for you? These adjustments can be just as valuable to you as a higher fee, while feeling to the brand like you're meeting them halfway. You protect your rate *and* the relationship.

**Bundle to create value.** If a brand genuinely can't hit your single-post rate, a multi-post package at a modest discount can be more attractive than simply lowering your price — and it can increase your total earnings. Frame it as adding value, not conceding: "My standard rate for one post is $2,000; I can do a package of three over two months for $5,400, which gives you sustained visibility at a better per-post rate." You've held your positioning and grown the deal.

**Add value instead of dropping price.** When they hesitate at your number, sweeten it without discounting your core work — offer an extra story, a longer posting window, some behind-the-scenes content they can use, or a testimonial for their marketing. These cost you little but feel substantial to the brand, making the deal more attractive while keeping your rate intact.

**Use silence.** This is the most underused tactic there is. After you state your rate, stop talking. Most creators get uncomfortable with the pause and rush to justify their number or offer a discount nobody asked for — which instantly weakens their position. Don't. State your rate, give a brief reason it's fair, then go quiet and let them respond. More often than you'd expect, they'll accept it or counter much closer to your target than if you'd filled the silence with concessions.

**Get everything in writing.** A verbal "yes" is worth nothing if it's disputed later. Before any work begins, get the full terms confirmed — deliverables, payment amount and schedule, timeline, usage rights, exclusivity, the revision process. If the brand doesn't send a contract, write a simple summary email of everything agreed and ask them to confirm. This is the bridge straight into Module 7, where we build the real contract.

#### Handling the seven objections you'll actually hear

Negotiation tactics are general; the pushback you'll hear is specific. There are seven sentences brands say over and over, and knowing how to respond to each — without flinching, and without slashing your rate — is what separates a pro from a creator who folds. Here's how to handle each one.

**"That's above our budget."** Don't drop your price; trade. "I understand — let's look at what we can adjust. We could reduce the deliverables, shorten the usage window, or drop the exclusivity to get this closer to your budget." You've held your rate and given them a path to yes.

**"Other creators charge way less."** This is designed to make you doubt yourself. Don't. Calmly: "I'm sure some do — engagement and audience fit vary widely across creators. My rate reflects my 6.8% engagement rate and an audience that's 82% your target demographic. If price is the priority over fit, I might not be the right partner — but if results are, this rate is grounded in what I deliver." You're not arguing, you're educating.

**"We can only do $X maximum."** Three options. If $X is at or above your floor, you can accept it — but ask for something in return (shorter usage, removed exclusivity, faster payment). If it's below your floor, trade scope: "At $X, here's the package that makes sense — one Reel, organic only, no exclusivity." If even that won't work, you walk politely. Knowing your floor is what makes this decision instant.

**"Can you be flexible on price?"** A trap question, because saying "yes" without conditions teaches them to discount you forever. The answer: "I have flexibility on scope, timing, and usage rights. What's most important to you so we can find a structure that works for both?" You've reframed flexibility around variables, not your fee.

**"We're offering great exposure."** This is a red flag, not a real offer. Stay polite, stay firm: "I appreciate that — but exposure isn't a currency I can pay rent with. Professional brands compensate creators fairly. If budget's a real constraint, I'm happy to discuss a reduced scope that fits — but I can't take this on for exposure alone." If they push past that, this isn't a partner worth having (more in Module 10).

**"This is our standard rate, take it or leave it."** Almost never literally true — it's a pressure tactic. "I understand you have standard rates, and I have a fair-market rate based on my audience and engagement. If there's no room to move on price, can we look at the scope to find something that works for both of us?" You've called the bluff politely and reopened the conversation.

**"Let me get back to you" — and then silence.** This is the stalled negotiation, the fourth Stage in the Assistant. Wait three to five business days, then send one warm, short follow-up that restates your interest and makes it easy to reply. If no answer to that, send one final friendly nudge after another week. Then move on — and add the brand to your list to revisit in a few months. A non-response usually means internal budget approval is in motion, or it's a no they haven't told you yet; either way, two follow-ups is the limit before you stop.

The pattern across all seven: don't drop your price reflexively, redirect to the variables you can trade, and frame your response as a professional standard rather than a defensive reaction. The Assistant gives you these as three labeled strategies — Stand Your Ground, Adjust Scope, or other approaches — so picking your response becomes picking your *strategy*, not just an email.

#### Your non-negotiables

Tactics flex; non-negotiables don't. Before any negotiation, know your hard lines — the things you won't trade away no matter the offer. These usually include a minimum payment (your floor), creative-control limits (you won't be fully scripted or made to claim things you can't stand behind — which ties directly to your Module 3 deal-breakers), usage rights you won't grant without serious extra pay (like unlimited or perpetual use), and exclusivity periods that would block too many other opportunities.

Being clear on these in advance is what lets you negotiate calmly, because you already know where the edges are. And when a non-negotiable comes up, frame it as a professional policy rather than a personal refusal. "I maintain a policy of not granting unlimited usage rights without compensation that reflects that extended value" lands very differently than "no, I won't do that." Framing your boundaries as standards makes them easy for a brand to respect — and makes you sound like the professional you are.

#### Red flags: when to hold firm or walk

Some responses in a negotiation aren't bumps to work through — they're signals to reconsider the whole deal. We go deeper on red flags in Module 10, but in the negotiation itself, watch for these.

A brand that **won't discuss payment upfront**, or wants to see content before talking money, is a problem — compensation is a fundamental term that gets settled before work begins. **Unlimited-everything for minimal pay** — ten posts, full usage, year-long exclusivity, total creative control, all for the price of a single post — tells you they don't understand or don't respect creator value. **"Exposure" instead of payment** — "think of the followers you'll gain" — is a red flag; exposure doesn't pay your bills, and a serious brand pays you. And a brand that **refuses to put anything in writing**, or gets defensive when you ask, is showing you how the rest of the relationship would go.

None of these mean you negotiate harder. They mean you calmly decline and move on, protected by the floor and the non-negotiables you set in advance.

#### Where this is going

You now know how to prepare so you negotiate from strength, the tactics that move a deal your way, the boundaries that keep you protected, and the warning signs that tell you to walk. Negotiate this way and you won't just earn more on each deal — you'll earn the respect that turns brands into repeat partners.

And when the negotiation lands on a "yes," there's one more step before you create anything: getting it all in writing, properly. That's Module 7 — contracts and the legal essentials that protect everything you just negotiated. Bring the deal you're working on, and let's lock it down.

### Video 6.4 — Your Non-Negotiables
**Type:** Core · **Target:** 5–6 min

Tactics are things you flex. Non-negotiables are the things you don't — ever. And knowing yours in advance is what lets you negotiate calmly instead of getting talked into something you'll regret.

[On screen: "Know your hard lines"]

[On screen: "Decide them BEFORE the call"]

The key is deciding these *before* you're in the conversation. Because in the heat of a negotiation, with a nice number dangling in front of you, it's easy to talk yourself into crossing a line. If you've already drawn it, you don't have to make that decision under pressure — you just hold it.

[On screen: "Frame as policy, not refusal"]

Now here's the technique that makes non-negotiables land well. Frame them as professional policies, not personal refusals. Watch the difference. "No, I won't do that" sounds defensive and personal. But: "I maintain a policy of not granting unlimited usage rights without compensation that reflects that extended value" — that sounds like a professional with standards. Same boundary, completely different reception. When you frame a limit as a business policy, brands respect it and work around it, instead of pushing on it.

[On screen: "Boundaries = strength"]

And counterintuitively, having firm boundaries makes brands take you *more* seriously, not less. When a brand sees you're willing to hold a line — even walk away — they often become more flexible, not less. Confidence in your value strengthens your position; willingness to accept anything weakens it.
