Trust is the assetChapter 3.0

Why "yes to everything" quietly kills your business.

In Module 2 you built a pipeline. As it produces, you'll face a good problem: more opportunities than you should take. And here's where many creators sabotage themselves — they say yes to everything, because every deal looks like money.

Your single most valuable asset isn't your follower count or even your content — it's your audience's trust. That trust is what turns your recommendation into a brand's sales. And it's fragile: years to build, and one partnership that doesn't fit can quietly erode it in a moment.

But vetting isn't only defensive. There's an offensive reason to be selective, and it's the one that grows your income: aligned partnerships simply perform better. When you promote something your audience genuinely wants, the content converts, the brand sees results, and you get rebooked at higher rates and referred.

Misaligned deals do the opposite — they flop, and that becomes part of your track record. Being selective isn't leaving money on the table; it's how you make more of it. Creators known for being discerning have audiences who trust them more — which is exactly why they can charge a premium.

→ Saying no to the wrong deals is how you earn the right to charge premium rates for the right ones. This module is your filter.

Is this deal right for me?Chapter 3.1

Five questions to ask yourself.

Before you even reply to a brand, run the opportunity through five questions about you and your audience. The first two are deal-breakers — fail them and it's a no, no matter the fee.

1
Deal-breaker

Does it align with my values & content style?

The simplest test: would my audience be surprised to see me promoting this? Surprise is a red flag. If it feels like something you'd naturally recommend anyway, green light.

2
Deal-breaker

Can I authentically recommend the product?

Ideally you already use and love it; at minimum, test it thoroughly first. If the quality isn't there or you wouldn't spend your own money on it, decline — whatever they're paying. Your recommendations are your currency.

3
Usefulness

Would my audience genuinely benefit?

Partnership content performs best when it's useful — solving a problem or speaking to a real interest. Audiences can tell the difference between genuine enthusiasm and a paycheck.

4
Direction

Does it fit my long-term brand strategy?

Every deal shapes how brands and audiences see you. Ask where you want to be in three years — and whether this moves you toward it or blurs your identity.

5
Gut-check

Am I comfortable with the whole company?

You're endorsing the business, not just the product. A quick search for the company name + "controversy" surfaces most of what you'd want to know before committing.

Is this actually a good deal?Chapter 3.2

The questions to ask the brand.

These surface whether it's a good deal — and you ask them before you commit, not after. You're not negotiating yet; you're gathering the facts you need to decide.

1What does a win look like?

Goals & success metrics

Awareness, traffic, or conversions? This tells you what to create and flags unrealistic expectations early.

2How much is yours?

Creative freedom

The best partnerships let you apply what you know about your audience. Scripting every word produces stiff content that flops.

3Does it fit my capacity?

Timeline

Concepts due, review window, post date, revision rounds. Easier to ask for time before yes than to scramble after.

4Spell it out exactly

Deliverables

How many posts, which platforms, what formats, plus events or extras. This one step kills scope creep before it starts.

→ Then get the three that decide what it's worth: usage rights, compensation structure, and exclusivity. Priced in M4, negotiated in M6, locked in M7.

Will my people actually care?Chapter 3.3

Judging real audience fit.

Match the brand's target customer against your actual audience — where your Module 1 snapshot pays off again. There are two layers, and the second is where the real money is.

1
Layer one · on paper

Demographics

Age, location, gender, interests vs. who the brand wants to reach. A mismatch doesn't automatically kill the deal — but it does mean an honest conversation with the brand and adjusted expectations.

2
Layer two · where the money is

Psychographics

Your audience's attitudes, values, and lifestyle. A brand can match perfectly on age and location and still be wrong — line up the numbers but not the alignment. Read your comments and DMs: what do these people value and want to solve?

→ A deal that fits psychographically performs better — and performance is your leverage. It's what gets you rebooked, referred, and paid more.
