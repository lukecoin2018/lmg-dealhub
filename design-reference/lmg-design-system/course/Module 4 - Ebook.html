The patternChapter 4.0

Why most creators undercharge.

Pricing is the part of this business creators get most wrong — almost always in the same direction: too low. There are three reasons, and naming them is the first step to fixing your rates.

One: they anchor to a single number they heard once — "$100 per 10K followers" — and treat it as a ceiling instead of a floor. Two: they price on follower count alone and ignore the thing that actually makes them valuable — engagement.

Three — the big one: they give away usage rights, whitelisting, and exclusivity for free, because nobody told them those things have a price. You quote a number for "a post," and the brand walks away with the right to run that content as paid ads for a year, at no extra cost.

That's not a small mistake — it's often the difference between a deal worth taking and one that isn't. This module fixes all three: what sets your rate, how to charge for everything of value, and how to defend your number when a brand pushes back.

Your baseline: followers, niche & engagement.

The mental model is familiar: roughly $100 per 10K followers for a single post — so 50K starts around $500. Treat that as a sanity-check floor, not your final number, because two factors move it significantly.

1
Lever one

Your niche

Not all audiences are worth the same. Finance, tech, and B2B command higher rates — high-value products, real buying power. Lifestyle and fashion are more competitive. But specialization beats breadth: a focused "sustainable home" creator out-earns a general lifestyle creator of the same size.

2
Lever two · your biggest one

Engagement

A creator whose audience interacts and buys is worth far more than a same-sized creator who's scrolled past. This is the number you calculated in Module 1 — and here's where it finally sets your price. Above average? You price above baseline, full stop.

Pricing your deliverables.

Now what you're making — because not all content costs the same to produce or delivers the same value. Platform matters: Instagram and TikTok use similar math; YouTube commands a premium (higher production, and it keeps working for months). Content type matters just as much:

0.5–0.7×StoriesA set of 3–5 frames usually counts as one unit.
1×Feed postYour baseline. A single static post.
1.5–2×Reels / videoReach potential plus the editing skill involved.

The calculator lets you mix all of these in one package — which mirrors how real deals work (a Reel plus a few Stories, not one thing in isolation). When a brand wants several deliverables, bundle them with a modest 10–20% package discount to make it attractive — but be crystal clear about what's included, and don't discount so hard you've undervalued the whole thing just to win it.

→ A package should reward volume, not punish you for it.

Usage rights, whitelisting & exclusivity.

The step the calculator literally labels "where most creators undervalue themselves." Everything here is value you can charge for — and most creators don't.

1Organic vs. paid

How they use it

Organic only is baseline. Paid advertising — real media spend behind your content — is worth substantially more. Creator content in ads outperforms brand ads.

230d · 90d · 1yr · perpetual

Duration

The longer the window, the more you charge. Perpetual is a red flag — price it like selling an asset, or decline.

3+30% floor

Whitelisting / Spark Ads

Ads run from your account, using your handle and credibility. +30% is the floor — for significant ad spend, negotiate toward 1.5–2× base.

4Scale by length

Exclusivity

Not working with competitors = giving up income. Get paid for it, and pin down exactly what counts as a "competitor" in writing.

→ This single step is the difference between getting paid like a hobbyist and getting paid like a professional.

From a number to a rangeChapter 4.4

Present a number you can defend.

First, two value-adds that sit outside the calculator — you apply them manually: rush work (faster than normal) justifies +25–50%; concepting & strategy is separate billable work, often a flat fee from ~$500. And watch scope: 2 revision rounds is standard, 3 is the ceiling, 4+ is a warning sign.

Quote a range, not a number

"A single Reel typically runs $1,500–$2,000, depending on usage rights and timeline." The bottom is your minimum for a straightforward project; the top accounts for rush, extensive usage, or complex deliverables. A range anchors the brand at your real value and gives you room to flex without seeming arbitrary.

Underneath the range is one number you keep private: your floor — the minimum below which a deal doesn't make sense. Know it cold before any conversation; it's the line that tells you when to walk away. And as a setup for negotiation, you'll open slightly above your target so you have room to move — we do that properly in Module 6.
