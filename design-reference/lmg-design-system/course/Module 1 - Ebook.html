WelcomeChapter 1.0

What this course will do for you.

Whether you have 15,000 followers or 300,000, one pattern holds at almost every level: the creators who build real, reliable income from brand partnerships aren't necessarily the biggest. They're the ones who treat partnerships like a business instead of taking whatever shows up in their inbox.

Most creators — including ones with serious audiences — operate reactively. They wait to be discovered. They quote a rate off the top of their head, or quietly accept whatever the brand offers. They hand over usage rights, exclusivity, and extra deliverables without charging for them, often without realizing those things have a price. They run one-off deals on a treadmill, and when a brand goes quiet, the income goes quiet too. None of that is a size problem. It's a systems problem.

This course fixes the systems. By the time you finish, you'll find and attract the specific brands you want, price your work with confidence and defend that price, pitch proactively and negotiate without flinching, protect yourself with contracts and disclosure you understand, deliver work that gets you rebooked, and — the big one — turn scattered one-off deals into stable, recurring income.

That's the destination. This first module is about getting your bearings: seeing the full range of ways you can get paid, understanding what actually drives what you can charge, and getting an honest baseline on where your partnership business stands today. Let's start with the money — because most creators are using only a fraction of what's available.

The moneyChapter 1.1

The six ways creators get paid.

Most creators know about sponsored posts and maybe affiliate links. There are six distinct revenue streams — and even established creators routinely leave several untouched. Knowing the full menu is the first step to monetizing your audience completely instead of partially.

1The familiar one

Sponsored content

A flat fee to create a post, video, or story featuring a brand. One piece of content, one payment. The craft is integration — making it feel native to your feed, not bolted on.

Flat fee · per piece
2Earns on autopilot

Affiliate partnerships

A commission on the sales you drive — usually 5–30% — via a unique link or code. Keeps earning long after you post. Stack a flat fee with commission for a baseline plus upside.

5–30% commission
3Usually the goal

Brand ambassadorships

A longer 6–12 month relationship where you become a recurring face for a brand. Trade a slice of your per-post rate for something better: predictable, recurring income.

6–12 months · recurring
4Use it early

Product gifting

Free product in exchange for content, no cash. Good for portfolio and relationships — but your time has value. As you grow, get selective and charge for the content anyway.

Product only
5Most commonly missed

Content licensing

Getting paid again for work you already made — when a brand reuses it in their email, on their site, or in their ads. Always carries payment on top of your original fee.

Add-on fee
6The ambitious tier

Co-creation

Building something with a brand — a product, a collection, a signature item — often an upfront fee plus ongoing royalties. Real time and creative input, real long-term income.

Fee + royalties
→ If you're only doing sponsored posts, sit with this: you may be monetizing a quarter of your real opportunity. We'll change that.

The leversChapter 1.2

What actually drives your rates.

If two creators have the same follower count, why does one command triple the rate? Because rates aren't set by size — they're set by five factors. Understanding them tells you exactly what this course is going to sharpen.

1
The biggest one

Engagement

Brands pay for an audience that interacts and buys, not one that scrolls past — so they study your likes-to-follower ratio, comment quality, replies, and click-throughs. Strong engagement is what lets you charge above baseline, at any size. (Bought followers are a dead end — brands detect it, and getting caught ends relationships across their whole network.)

2
Your calling card

Content quality

Your eye, your storytelling, your ability to make something a brand's in-house team can't. Not about expensive gear — about work that looks intentional and consistent. Better content raises the caliber of brand willing to work with you, and the rate you can ask.

3
Most underrated

Professionalism

The easiest lever to win on. Brands deal with creators who reply late, miss deadlines, or vanish mid-campaign. Be the reliable, clear, easy-to-work-with partner and you get rebooked and referred — sometimes that matters more than the size of your audience.

4
It compounds

Track record

Results you've driven, content you've made, words from past partners — each deal makes the next one easier and better paid. Early, smaller deals are worth taking specifically to build this proof.

5
Ties it together

Audience understanding

Not just how many people follow you, but who they are and what they buy. The more precisely you can show a brand that your audience is their customer, the more bookable — and more expensive — you become.

Your baselineChapter 1.3

Your pricing-power number.

The fastest way to see where you stand is your engagement rate — and it takes about five minutes. Take a typical recent post. Add the likes and comments together, divide by your follower count, multiply by 100.

The calculation
( Likes + CommentsFollowers ) × 100 = Your rate
2–3%
Average
3–5%
Good
5%+
Excellent
Worked example

1,500 likes + 80 comments ÷ 22,000 followers × 100 = 7.2% — excellent, and a strong argument for premium pricing. Run that same calculation on a 300,000-follower account with proportional engagement and the conclusion is identical: this is the number that justifies your price.

A creator at 6% engagement can defend a meaningfully higher rate than a same-sized creator at 1% — because the brand is paying for an audience that actually responds. Whatever your number, it's not a verdict; it's a baseline and a lever. We build your actual rates on top of it in Module 4.

→ While you're in your analytics, grab your audience snapshot too — age ranges, top locations, gender split, interests. You'll use it in your media kit, your pitches, and every brand-fit decision from here on.
