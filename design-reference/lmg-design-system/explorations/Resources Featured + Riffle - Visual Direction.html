Does the blend cohere?

Yes — with guardrails

The blend holds together because the two halves are given different jobs and different energy levels, not the same one twice. The featured cover is larger (270px) and sits in dead-still space with a full text block, so the eye lands there first; the riffle's front cover is deliberately smaller (200px), tilted, and embedded in a receding stack, so it reads as "a library in motion" rather than a second hero competing for the headline. The single dotted rule between them, and the pink accent reserved for the riffle's progress and caption, keep the two zones legible.

The guardrails that make it work — and that we would not relax: keep the featured slot perfectly still (any drift there and the two sides start fighting); cap the auto-advance at ~2.6s (faster reads as frantic next to a calm anchor); and keep the riffle's front cover smaller than the featured cover. If a future layout needs the featured cover to shrink, the blend tips toward clutter — that is the failure mode to watch.

Honest note on motion cost: the auto-riffle shown here is production-cheap (CSS transforms driven by a timer, plus click-to-front). The truly tactile version from the brief — grab the stack and flick through it with inertia — is the expensive part: it needs pointer-velocity tracking and a small spring/decay model, and careful touch handling so it doesn't hijack page scroll on mobile. Recommend shipping the auto + click version first, then layering drag-to-riffle as a progressive enhancement.

Below: the calmer fallback — Direction 4 as originally designed (featured + two quietly drifting columns) — so you can compare side by side. It is the safer pick if the riffle ever feels too busy in context, or for a first ship while the drag physics are budgeted.
