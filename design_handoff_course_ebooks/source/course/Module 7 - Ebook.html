The reframeChapter 7.0

Contracts are protection, not paperwork.

The negotiation lands on a yes. There's one thing between you and starting work — the thing most creators rush or skip: get the deal in writing, properly.

A contract turns the conversation you won in Module 6 into terms you can enforce. It protects you from scope creep, late payment, and a brand using your content in ways you never agreed to. And it protects the brand too — which is why one that resists putting things in writing is telling you something important.

The good news: you don't write contracts from scratch, and you don't need a law degree. The Contract Builder gives you a lawyer-grade starting document for any of seven deal types. This module gives you the understanding underneath it — what each section protects, and how to spot a clause that's quietly bad for you.

Every contract has eight standard sections — six required, two optional. Let's go through them.

The foundationChapter 7.1

The eight sections · 1–4.

1
Parties & ScopeRequired
Who is in the contract — and that changes your legal exposure. Individual creator = your personal name & assets; a business entity carries the exposure instead. Scope is the one-line "what this is about."
2
Deliverables & TimelineRequired
Your shield against scope creep. Platform, count, content type, due date, specs — plus the approval cadence and minimum days live (standard 30) so they can't pull a post early or sit on your draft.
3
Payment TermsRequired
≥50% upfront for new brands · Net 30 standard (shorter is better) · late fee ~1.5%/month gives them a reason to pay on time · payment method in writing.
4
Usage Rights & LicensingCritical
Where creators leak the most value. Limit duration (1 year default; perpetual = 2–3× rate). Specify platforms & organic-vs-paid. Never "general marketing purposes." You keep ownership; they get a license.
→ Usage rights are the same place you leaked value in Module 4 pricing — now you lock the language so what you negotiated stays yours.

The protective layersChapter 7.2

The eight sections · 5–8.

5
Revisions & ApprovalsRequired
2–3 rounds standard. Define what counts: a caption tweak is a revision; reshooting a Reel is new work, billed separately. Cap the brand's approval turnaround so their delay isn't your problem.
6
Exclusivity TermsOptional
Never free — priced in Module 4. Pin down specific competitor categories ("refillable cleaning products," not "any home-care brand") and a capped period (campaign + 30 days).
7
Kill Fee & TerminationOptional
The one creators forget until they need it. 25–50% if cancelled after work begins, 100% after delivery. Include it on anything over a few hundred dollars.
8
FTC Compliance & DisclosuresRequired
Non-negotiable. Clearly disclose any material connection — paid, gifted, affiliate. Fines fall on you. The section the brand may try to soften — don't let them.
→ Six required, two optional — and FTC is the one that's always required and always worth getting exactly right. Next page: how.

The layer underneath your dealsChapter 7.4

The business basics.

Two practical notes that sit outside the contract but matter for the same reason — protection.

1Legal separation

Form an LLC

Once you're earning real partnership revenue (~$20K+/yr), an LLC insulates your personal assets from lawsuits and debt. A few hundred dollars and a couple of hours via an accountant or a formation service.

2Set aside 25–30%

Taxes

You owe income and self-employment tax. Set aside 25–30% of every payment in a separate account, track every business expense, and consider quarterly estimated payments to avoid penalties.

The simplest thing you can do today

Open a separate bank account for business income and expenses — so at tax time you're not untangling a year of personal-and-business mixing. Track equipment, software, props, a portion of phone & internet, and professional services; they all reduce your taxable income.

⚖️ Educational, not legal or tax advice. For your specific situation, country, and income level, consult a qualified attorney or accountant — the investment pays back several times over.
